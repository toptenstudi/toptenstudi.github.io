1. a. Metal Instruments - Gangsa Toppaya, Gangsa Palook Gangsa Toppaya Gangsa Palook​sana masagutan nyo


Answer:

Gangsa

Explanation:

The gangsa is a well-known musical instruments of the Cordillerans. It is a flat metal gong which are played in an ensemble. There are two styles of playing the gangsa; either Gangsa Toppaya or the Gangsa Palook.

In Gangsa Toppaya style, the musicians play or stikes the surface of the gangsa with their bare hand while in a sitting position, with a single gangsa resting on the lap of each musician. The ensemble accompanies a pair of dancers mimicking a rooster (male) courting a hen (female).

In the Gangsa Pattung style, a gangsa is suspended from the musician's left hand and played with a padded stick held in the musician's right hand. In the "pattung" style of playing, the players are standing, or they keep in step with the dancers while bending forward slightly. The ensemble accompanies group of dancers usually in line following their leaders signals in creating different steps of the dance.

what place does the musical instrument "gangsa"originated with​?

A gangsa (not to be mistaken for the Balinese instrument with a similar name) is native to the way of life found in the mountain locales (the Cordillera) of the northern Philippines. The gangsa of the northern Philippines is a solitary hand-held smooth-surfaced gong with a restricted edge. A bunch of gangsa, which is played one gong for each artist, comprises of gangsa tuned to various notes, contingent upon provincial or neighborhood social inclinations. The quantity of gangsa in a set fluctuates with accessibility, and relies upon the practice of a specific ethnic gathering of the Luzon Cordillera: Kalinga, Ifugao, Bontoc, and so forth

Among the Kalinga individuals in the Cordillera locale of Luzon Island, the gangsa is played in two ways. One way is classified "toppaya" and the other is designated "pattung." In "toppaya" style, the artists play the outer layer of the gangsa with their hand while in a sitting situation, with a solitary gangsa laying on the lap of every performer. In the "pattung" style, a gangsa is suspended from the artist's left hand and played with a cushioned stick held in the performer's right hand. In the "pattung" way of playing, the players are standing, or they keep in sync with the artists while bowing forward marginally.
