About neutron capture

Neutron capture is a nuclear reaction in which an atomic nucleus and one or more neutrons collide and combine to form a heavier nucleus. Because neutrons have no electric charge, they can enter the nucleus more easily than positively charged protons, which are repelled by electrostatics.

Neutron capture plays an important role in the cosmic nucleosynthesis of heavy elements. In stars, this reaction can proceed in two ways: as a fast process (r process) or a slow process (s process). Nuclei with a mass greater than 56 cannot be formed by thermonuclear reactions (ie by nuclear fusion), but can be formed by capture of neutrons.

Neutron capture at a small neutron flux

In a small neutron flux, as in a nuclear reactor, a single neutron is captured by the nucleus. For example, when natural gold (197Au) is irradiated by neutrons, the isotope 198Au is then formed in a highly excited state, and rapidly decays to the ground state 198Au while emitting γ rays. In this process, the mass number increases by one.

This reaction is written with a formula in the form 197Au+n → 198Au+γ, or in the abbreviated form 197Au(n,γ)198Au.

If thermal neutrons are used, this process is called thermal capture.

The isotope 198Au (Z=79) is a beta emitter that decays to become the mercury isotope 198Hg (Z=80). In this process the atomic number (Z) increases by one.

Neutron capture at high neutron flux

In the r process that occurs inside stars if the neutron flux density is so high that the atomic nucleus does not have time to decay through beta emission between neutron captures.
