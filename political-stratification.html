Dependency theory was created in part as a response to the Western-centric mindset of modernization theory. It states that global inequality is primarily caused by core nations (or high-income nations) exploiting semi-peripheral and peripheral nations (or middle-income and low-income nations), which creates a cycle of dependence (Hendricks 2010). As long as peripheral nations are dependent on core nations for economic stimulus and access to a larger piece of the global economy, they will never achieve stable and consistent economic growth. In modern Western societies, social stratification is typically defined in terms of three social classes: the upper class, the middle class, and the lower class; in turn, each class can be subdivided into the upper-stratum, the middle-stratum, and the lower stratum.[4] Moreover, a social stratum can be formed upon the bases of kinship, clan, tribe, or caste, or all four.

The categorization of people by social stratum occurs most clearly in complex state-based, polycentric, or feudal societies, the latter being based upon socio-economic relations among classes of nobility and classes of peasants. Whether social stratification first appeared in hunter-gatherer, tribal, and band societies or whether it began with agriculture and large-scale means of social exchange remains a matter of debate in the social sciences.[5] Determining the structures of social stratification arises from inequalities of status among persons, therefore, the degree of social inequality determines a person's social stratum. Generally, the greater the social complexity of a society, the more social stratification exists, by way of social differentiation.[6]

Answer:

Social and political stratification refers to the way in which a society is organized into hierarchical layers or strata, with some individuals or groups having more power, privilege, and access to resources than others. This can manifest in various forms, such as class, race, gender, and other forms of social inequality.

Reflecting on social and political stratification can help to understand and raise awareness about the ways in which different forms of inequality and oppression are perpetuated and reinforced in society. It can also help to identify the ways in which privilege and power are distributed, and how this can lead to disparities in access to resources, opportunities, and basic human rights.

One important aspect of social and political stratification is that it is often reinforced by systemic barriers and discrimination. This can be seen in the way that certain groups are denied access to education, employment, and other opportunities, or how they are disproportionately targeted by law enforcement and the criminal justice system.

Another aspect is that, social and political stratification is not only restricted to the individual level but also to the societal level. The way in which a society is organized, in terms of its political and economic systems, can also reinforce and perpetuate inequality and oppression.

Reflecting on social and political stratification can also help to identify and challenge the ways in which individuals and groups are marginalized and excluded from society, and to work towards creating more inclusive and equitable communities.

In conclusion, reflecting on social and political stratification can help to understand the ways in which different forms of inequality and oppression are perpetuated and reinforced in society, and to identify and challenge the ways in which individuals and groups are marginalized and excluded from society. It can also lead to the creation of more inclusive and equitable communities
