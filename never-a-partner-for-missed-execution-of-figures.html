26. A figure skater lifts his partner 2 m off the ground with an acceleration of 2.5m/s2. if the figure skater exerts a force of 200N , what is the mass of the partner?


Hello!

A figure skater lifts his partner 2 m off the ground with an acceleration of 2.5m/s2. if the figure skater exerts a force of 200N , what is the mass of the partner ?

We have the following data:

a (acceleration) = 2.5 m/s²

F (force) = 200 N (or kg.m/s²)

m (mass) = ? (in kg)

We apply the data to the resulting Force Formula, we have:

[tex]\boxed{F = m * a}[/tex]

Solving:

[tex]F = m*a \to m = \dfrac{F}{a}[/tex]

[tex]m = \dfrac{200\:kg*m/s^2\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\dfrac{\hspace{0.6cm}}{~}}{2.5\:m/s^2\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\dfrac{\hspace{0.6cm}}{~}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{\boxed{m = 80\:kg}}\Longleftarrow(mass)\end{array}}\qquad\checkmark[/tex]

Answer:

The mass of the partner is 80 kg

29. Why we never blame our partner for missed execution?​


Answer:

The biggest problem is how it affects the person who blames. Blame affects people in many ways. Research shows that people who blame others lose status, learn less, and perform worse relative to others. In particular…Blame creates inaction. When someone blames, it’s as if they’re handing over control of the situation. “I can’t change until you do,” is the implicit message. The solution is in their partner’s hands.
