10. 1. Directions: Using the Venn diagram, compare and contrast the characteristics of informative, persuasive and argumentative writing.​


Answer:

Sure, I can help you compare and contrast the characteristics of informative, persuasive, and argumentative writing using a Venn diagram.

| Informative Writing | Persuasive Writing | Argumentative Writing |

| --- | --- | --- |

| Presents factual information | Presents an opinion or argument | Presents a claim and supports it with evidence |

| Focuses on educating the reader | Aims to convince the reader to take a certain action or adopt a certain viewpoint | Aims to persuade the reader to agree with the writer's claim |

| Avoids bias and emotion | Uses emotional appeals and may be biased | Uses logical appeals and evidence to support the claim |

| Provides background information and context | Presents the writer's opinion or stance on an issue | Presents both sides of an issue and refutes counterarguments |

| May include descriptive writing and storytelling | Uses rhetorical devices such as repetition and rhetorical questions | Uses logical fallacies and refutes opposing arguments |

As you can see from the Venn diagram, there are some similarities and differences between informative, persuasive, and argumentative writing. Informative writing focuses on presenting factual information, while persuasive writing aims to convince the reader to take a certain action or adopt a certain viewpoint. Argumentative writing takes persuasive writing a step further by presenting a claim and supporting it with evidence and logical appeals. All three types of writing may use rhetorical devices, but persuasive writing is more likely to use emotional appeals and biased language, while argumentative writing uses logical appeals and refutes counterarguments.

The persuasive purpose is used to convince, or persuade, the reader that the opinion, or assertion, or claim, of the writer is correct or valid. Persuasion is more selfish than argument (debate). Argument attempts to arrive at a logical solution to an issue. But persuasive writing is concerned primarily with convincing the reader at any cost. Various appeals are used to manipulate the reader. Because of its goal, the persuasive purpose is usually the primary purpose in a piece of writing. The author may supplement the persuasive purpose with any of the other purposes.
