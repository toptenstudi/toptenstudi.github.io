2. Singles serve and receive in the Answer service court. (table tennis)


SERVE AND RECEIVE IN SINGLES (TABLE TENNIS)The rule in the service in the singles of table tennis is that, a player can serve to and from any part of the court.

However, in the doubles match of table tennis, the serve must be always played diagonally, i.e within the right half side of the court only.

In table tennis, once the ball has bounce in your side of the court after striking, it has to pass over (or around) the net and hit your opponent's side of the court.

In table tennis, the ball is only allowed to bounce once in each side of the court.

In the instance that your opponent or opponents is or are not able to strike back after it has bounced once, you win the point. You win the point unless it touched the net when you served --- then you have to replay the point again.

9. The Answer service is always from the right service court to the opponent's right service court. (table tennis)


Answer:

This rule is applied if you are playing doubles in table tennis.

Explanation:

As a general rule in doubles in table tennis, the service must bounce first to the right side of the server's playing end and must bounce on the right side of the receiver's playing end. After that, the receiver may return the ball anywhere he wants to get a point from the opponent. It is very critical for the server of both teams to keep the service within the service areas because a service error may be called and a point will be awarded to the opponent. A thin line across the table with a width of 3mm (1/8 cm) is used to determine whether the ball stays in the right side or not. With the current rule of thumb in playing doubles, if the ball hits the center line, automatically it will be counted to the right side so the receiver must return the ball to get a point.

Answer:

1.A.Low serve

The low serve is played gently over the net to land at the front of your opponent's service court, near the short service line. It's important that this serve passes low over the net (hence the name low serve ).

2.D.Drive serve

3C.Flick serve

The flick serve is also played upwards, but much more shallowly than the high serve. The idea is to deprive the opponent of time, forcing him to hit the shuttlecock when it's behind his body. Flick serves are used extensively in doubles, and occasionally in singles.

4.B.High serve

The high serve is played powerfully upwards, so that the shuttlecock travels very high and falls almost vertically downwards at the back of the receiver's service court.
