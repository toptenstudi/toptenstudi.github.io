3. Strategies Question Communication ​


Answer:

What happened at the meeting?

Why did he react that way?

How was the party?

Tell me what happened next.

Describe the circumstances in more detail.

Open questions are good for:

Developing an open conversation: "What did you get up to on vacation?"

Finding out more detail: "What else do we need to do to make this a success?"

Finding out the other person's opinion or issues: "What do you think about those changes?"

Closed questions are good for:

Testing your understanding, or the other person's: "So, if I get this qualification, I will get a raise?"

Concluding a discussion or making a decision: "Now we know the facts, are we all agreed this is the right course of action?"

Frame setting: "Are you happy with the service from your bank?"

16. questions about communicative strategies


Answer:

1. How will the company respond to leaks in the business media or rumors? Many financial media outlets, which often rely on unnamed sources, are quick to predict who is next to fail. Such media speculation can cost sales, weaken supply chains and distract and disrupt employees and operations — all at a time the company already is under pressure.

2. Are the company’s messages — both internal and external — flexible enough to allow for varied contingencies as the restructuring process unfolds? Restructurings evolve quickly and take unexpected turns. The best communications plans anticipate varied scenarios, from M&A to a quietly negotiated restructuring. Their messages allow the flexibility to change courses without risking litigation or a loss of credibility. They strike a balance of the company saying enough so that suspicion isn’t found in silence, and not too much because sometimes less is more.

3. Is the company ready to stand in the spotlight? Does the company have the communications infrastructure to manage the risks of engaging (or not engaging) with media and in direct communications to key audiences? What are the expectations for your communications among the people who matter most to the business – employees, shareholders, customers, suppliers? Does the company have the resources, practices and culture that are required to support the added burdens of a restructuring?

4. Do employees and other vital stakeholders (from shareholders to creditors) buy into the board’s and management’s strategies for getting through the immediate issues? Employees are the external face of the company – can management empower them as ambassadors without losing control of the message? The most powerful driver of opinion is often the voice of respected third parties – analysts to academics. What will they say when the company makes an announcement? And can the company move early to freshen up its key relationships, helping the various stakeholders understand where the company’s heading and why?

5. Is the company keeping in sight its long game? The most successful restructurings start with a vision for the long term. Companies have more success when their audiences have a strong sense of the company’s future vision, strategies and opportunities. Stakeholders — from employees to investors — tend to be more willing to suffer short-term pain if they truly believe in a more attractive future state. Knowing where you’re going — and being able to articulate the plan — should be an early step in any effort to restructure.
