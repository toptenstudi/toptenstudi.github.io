1. crossing the bar questions and answers


Crossing the bar is a poem by Alfred Lord Tennyson.

    A metaphorical meditation on death in which the speaker likens passing through the sandbar between a coastal region and the larger sea or ocean to dying or passing through it in a particular way

Questions:

What significance does the phrase "clear call" have to the poet, according to your analysis of Crossing the Bar?What wishes does the poet have for his last journey?What does it mean to be "moaning"?What is the bar the poet is attempting to cross?Why does he want to cross the bar?

Answers:

The poet claims that the sun setting is a direct invitation for him to return to his true home, which is life after death.Tennyson writes about the sandbars, where the waves break. He utilizes it as a metaphor for a person's dying moments, when most individuals get depressed and seek to postpone their passing.The poet conveys his desire to depart without complaint or regret. Sandbars form near the mouths of rivers and harbors, and one must disturb and crush the sandbars in order to embark on a sea voyage.The only thing standing between the poet and the sea is a sandbar, which he longs to cross. Beyond him is a wide sea that has a known endpoint but an uncertain route. In order to cruise over the water without any interruptions or obstructions, he wants the sea level to rise.In order to continue his journey, he wants to cross the bar, before he arrives at his destiny and encounters God, his maker.
