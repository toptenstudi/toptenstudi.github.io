The speaker begins by emerging from a metaphorical night that lies on top of the speaker like a physical thing. This night, which seems to fill the whole world, is as dark as Hell. Despite this darkness, the speaker feels gratitude towards any god or gods that may exist for granting unshakeable resilience.

Looking back on life's past challenges, which constrained the speaker like a giant fist, the speaker remembers never showing discomfort or complaining. Going even further, the speaker compares life's unexpected mishaps to being beaten with a heavy implement. The speaker was damaged by this beating, yet that fact did not decrease the speaker's pride or resolve.

Now the speaker looks beyond the present of anger and sadness to the future. Unfortunately, the future's only certainty is death, which hangs over the present like a terrifying shadow. However, the speaker once again affirms that the threat of inevitable suffering does not, and never will, frighten the speaker.

The speaker doesn't care how challenging life becomes, alluding to a biblical passage in which a narrow gate represents extreme difficulty. Nor does the speaker care how many horrible events lie in the book of fate. The speaker controls the course of their own inner life. Like a ship's captain, the speaker remains in charge of their inner life's unconquerable element: the soul.

12. formalist approach on "invictus" ​


Answer:

The formalist approach to literary analysis focuses on the form, structure, language, and other elements of a text as they exist within the text itself, rather than looking at the author's life or social or historical context. In this approach, the meaning of the text arises from the way these elements work together.

In the poem "Invictus" by William Ernest Henley, the form and language of the poem are notable for their directness and simplicity. The poem consists of four stanzas, each with four lines. The rhyme scheme is ABAB, and the meter is mostly iambic tetrameter, with occasional variations. The poem also makes use of repetition, with the phrase "I am" repeated three times in the first two stanzas.

The poem's language is straightforward and declarative, with short, simple sentences. The speaker asserts his own strength and independence, despite the challenges he has faced, and emphasizes his own willpower and determination. The language is also highly visual, with vivid images such as "the fell clutch of circumstance" and "the bludgeonings of chance."

One key element of the poem is its use of the first person point of view. The speaker asserts his own agency and autonomy, claiming that he is the master of his fate and the captain of his soul. This emphasis on the individual's power and control is in keeping with the formalist emphasis on the text itself, rather than on external factors such as history or biography.

Overall, the formalist approach to "Invictus" focuses on the poem's form, structure, and language as they contribute to its meaning. The poem's simple, direct style and emphasis on the individual's strength and willpower contribute to its enduring appeal and resonance.

14. whai is the background or an interpretation of the poem invictus?​


The background of the poem Invictus

Invictus, a poem that continues to burn, uncontrollably, does not fade with time, like a promise in the heart that will always be kept. This poem was written by William Ernest Henley (1849–1903) in 1875.

In 1962 Nelson Mandela was arrested by the South African government after several years he fought and carried out a revolution in South Africa. Nelson Mandela in his solitude in prison and in his struggle for justice in South Africa, always remembers Invictus's poems and this is one of his motivations to keep fighting for justice for all black people in South Africa.

We can see from Invictus and the experiences of the two characters we saw above, that true strength does not come from how we can defeat the enemy or become richer than everyone. But we see that the real strength comes from our hearts and souls, how all circumstances cannot defeat us, and how we can stay strong and not submit to the fate that wants to take us to the wrong place.

Interpretation of Invictus poetryIn the first stanza, the author prays in the dark for an existing god, to thank him for the power of his soul. Here are some things that seem obvious: that the person is faced with "personal darkness" or despair and secondly, that despite this despair, he feels strong, and for that he is grateful.In the second verse, the author continues to make references to courage. He talks about how despite being in a negative state, he doesn't let himself down and that even though fate hasn't given his best, he remains straight and uncomplaining.The third verse deals with death and the previous absence of fear. Transmits that apart from anger and crying, things can't be with him yet.The fourth stanza summarizes the main theme of the poem: that no matter the circumstances, how hard the road and negative events occur. The author will always be the one to guide his path: the "master of his destiny" and the "captain of his soul".
