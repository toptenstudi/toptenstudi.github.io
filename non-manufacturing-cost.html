17. When using variable costing, fixed manufacturing overhead costs are best described as? A. indirect period costs b. indirect product costs


When using variable costing, fixed manufacturing overhead costs are best described as indirect product costs.

Indirect costs include things like supplies, utilities, office equipment rentals, desktop computers, and cell phones. Similar to direct expenses, indirect costs can be either fixed or variable. A constant indirect cost would be rent; variable indirect costs would be things that alter, like gas and electricity.

Regardless of the quantity produced or sold, indirect costs are the expenses associated with operating a firm and entering the market with a good or service. In other words, they have nothing to do with producing a good or service or purchasing a good in bulk to resell. This sets them apart from direct costs.

Indirect manufacturing expenses are those expenditures incurred during production that aren't directly related to a produced unit. A few examples of these costs are supplies, depreciation, utilities, production supervisor wages, and equipment upkeep. When overhead costs are pooled together into an inventory asset and dispersed to the quantity produced over the course of a reporting period, indirect manufacturing costs are capitalized into the asset.

29. Under variable costing, fixed manufacturing overhead is treated as a product cost.


Fixed Manufacturing Overhead

By excluding fixed industrial overhead from the product-cost of production, variable costing is a managerial and cost accounting concept. Unlike absorption costing, which allocates the fixed production overhead to the items produced, this method does not do this.

FALSE

Under variable costing, fixed overhead is not regarded as a product cost. Fixed manufacturing overhead is still recorded as an item on the income statement, but it is now done so as a cost that is deducted from each period's revenue.

Supporting details:

Only the variable production costs are accounted for when using variable costing. The costs that are inventoried do not include selling and administrative charges because they are not considered to be product costs.

Fixed manufacturing overhead costs are handled as period costs under variable costing, thus they are always expensed during the period in which they are incurred. Regardless of the costing methodology employed, all other costs are regarded equally, hence profit is the same when the quantity of units produced and sold is equal.

Variable costing treats fixed manufacturing overhead as a period cost, whereas absorption costing treats it as a product cost (included in inventories on the balance sheet until sold) (expensed on the income statement as incurred).
