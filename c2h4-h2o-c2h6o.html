3. balance the following equations ? CH4 + O2 CO2 + H2O CaCl2 + AgNO3- Ca(NO3)2 + AgCl C2H6O + O2 CO2 + H2O


Further explanation  

Equalization of chemical reactions (balanced chemical equations) can be done using variables. Steps in equalizing the reaction equation:  

1. gives a coefficient on substances involved in the equation of reaction such as a, b, or c, etc.  2. make an equation based on the similarity of the number of atoms where the number of atoms = coefficient × index (subscript) between reactant and product  3. Select the coefficient of the substance with the most complex chemical formula equal to 1  

1. CH₄ + O₂ ⇒ CO₂ + H₂O

CH₄ + aO₂ -----> bCO₂ + cH₂O  

C: left = 1, right = b -> b = 1  

H: left = 4, right = 2c ---> 4 = 2c ---> c = 2  

O: left = 2a, right = 2b + c ---> 2a = 2b + c ---> 2a = 2.1 + 2 -> 2a = 4 -> a = 2  

The equation becomes:  

CH₄ + 2 O₂ -----> CO₂ + 2 H₂O  

2. CaCl₂ + AgNO₃ ⇒Ca(NO₃)₂ + AgCl

aCaCl₂ + bAgNO₃ ⇒Ca(NO₃)₂ + cAgCl

Ca : left = a , right = 1 ⇒ a=1

Cl : left = 2, right = c ⇒ c=2

Ag : left = b, right = c  ⇒ b=c = 2

NO₃ : left = b, right = 2 ⇒ b=2

The equation becomes:  

CaCl₂ + 2AgNO₃ ⇒Ca(NO₃)₂ + 2AgCl

3. C₂H₆O + O₂ ⇒ CO₂ + H₂O

C₂H₆O + aO₂ ⇒ bCO₂ + cH₂O

C : left=2, right =b ⇒ b=2

H : left =6, right = 2c ⇒ 2c=6 ⇒c=3

O : left =2a+1, right=2b+c ⇒2a+1=2b+c⇒2a+1=2.2+3⇒2a=6⇒a=3

The equation becomes:  

C₂H₆O + 3O₂ ⇒ 2CO₂ + 3H₂O

9. Calculate the mole fraction of the solute in the following solutions: a. 100.0 grams C2H6O in 100.0 grams H2O


Solution:

Step 1: Calculate the molar masses of solute and solvent.

For C₂H₆O (solute)

molar mass = (12.01 g/mol × 2) + (1.008 g/mol × 6) + (16.00 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 46.07 g/mol

For H₂O (solvent)

molar mass = (1.008 g/mol × 2) + (16.00 g/mol × 1)

molar mass = 18.016 g/mol

Step 2: Calculate the number of moles of solute and solvent.

For C₂H₆O (solute)

[tex]n_{\text{solute}} = \text{100.0 g C₂H₆O} × \frac{\text{1 mol C₂H₆O}}{\text{46.07 g C₂H₆O}}[/tex]

[tex]n_{\text{solute}} = \text{2.171 mol}[/tex]

For H₂O (solvent)

[tex]n_{\text{solvent}} = \text{100.0 g H₂O} × \frac{\text{1 mol H₂O}}{\text{18.016 g H₂O}}[/tex]

[tex]n_{\text{solvent}} = \text{5.551 mol}[/tex]

Step 3: Calculate the mole fraction of solute.

[tex]X_{\text{solute}} = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{n_{\text{solute}} \: + \: n_{\text{solvent}}}[/tex]

[tex]X_{\text{solute}} = \frac{{\text{2.171 mol}}{\text{2.171 mol} \: + \: \text{5.551 mol}}[/tex]

[tex]\boxed{X_{\text{solute}} = 0.28114}[/tex]

23. Determine ΔH for the reaction 2 CH4 → 2 H2 + C2H4 given these data:CH4 + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O ΔH = −891 kJC2H4 + 3O2 → 2 CO2 + 2 H2O ΔH = −1,411 kJ2H2 + O2 → 2 H2O ΔH = −571 kJ​


[tex]\tt{\huge{\blue{Explanation:}}}[/tex]

Our goal here is to calculate the enthalpy change for the decomposition of CH₄ into C₂H₄ and H₂. The reaction does not occur directly, however, so we must use an indirect route using the information given by Equations (a), (b), and (c). The equations are:

(a) CH₄ + 2 O₂ → C₂H₄ + 2 H₂O ∆H = –891 kJ

(b) C₂H₄ + 3O₂ → 2 CO₂ + 2 H₂O ∆H = –1,411 kJ

(c) 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O ∆H = -571 kJ

The overall reaction is

2 CH₄ → 2 H₂ + C₂H₄

[tex]\tt{\huge{\red{Solution:}}}[/tex]

Looking at the overall reaction, we need 2 moles of CH₄ as a reactant so we multiply Equation (a) by 2 to get

(d) [tex]\begin{array}{cccccccl} 2\text{CH}_4 & + & 4\text{O}_2 & \rightarrow & 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 4\text{H}_2\text{O} & \Delta H = 2(-\text{891 kJ}) \\ & & & & & & & \Delta H = -\text{1,782 kJ} \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Looking at the overall reaction, we need 1 mole of C₂H₄ as a product. So we reverse Equation (b) to get

(e) [tex]\begin{array}{cccccccl} 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 2\text{H}_{2}\text{O} & \rightarrow & \text{C}_2\text{H}_4 & + & 3\text{O}_2 & \Delta H = -(-\text{1,411 kJ}) \\ & & & & & & & \Delta H = \text{1,411 kJ} \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Looking at the overall reaction, we need 1 mole of H₂ as a product. So we reverse Equation (c) to get

(f) [tex]\begin{array}{cccccl} 2\text{H}_2\text{O} & \rightarrow & 2\text{H}_2 & + & \text{O}_2 & \Delta H = -(-\text{571 kJ}) \\ & & & & & \Delta H = \text{571 kJ} \\ \end{array}[/tex]

Adding Equations (d), (e), and (f) together, we get

[tex]\begin{array}{cccccccl} 2\text{CH}_4 & + & 4\text{O}_2 & \rightarrow & 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 4\text{H}_2\text{O} & \Delta H = -\text{1,782 kJ} \\ 2\text{CO}_2 & + & 2\text{H}_{2}\text{O} & \rightarrow & \text{C}_2\text{H}_4 & + & 3\text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \: \: \: \: \text{1,411 kJ} \\ & & 2\text{H}_2\text{O} & \rightarrow & 2\text{H}_2 & + & \text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \quad \: \: \: \text{571 kJ} \\ \hline & & 2\text{CH}_4 & \rightarrow & 2\text{H}_2 & + & \text{O}_2 & \Delta H = \quad \: \: \: \text{200 kJ} \end{array}[/tex]

Therefore, the heat of reaction is

[tex]\boxed{\Delta H = \text{200 kJ}}[/tex]
