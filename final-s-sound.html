8. The type of wave shown below is a longitudinal wave. This can be determined because the wave has compressions and rarefactions, which are characteristics of longitudinal waves.

9. The loudness of a sound is described by its intensity.

10. The speed of sound in air at -20° C is 319.5 m/s. This can be determined by using the following formula: speed = square root of (gamma * R * T), where gamma is the ratio of specific heats, R is the gas constant, and T is the temperature in Kelvin.

11. The most probable change of sound on an organ pipe would be an increase in wavelength if the person blows slightly harder. This would result in a lower frequency (pitch) and a longer wavelength.

12. Sound can travel through solids, liquids, and gases. This is because sound waves are mechanical waves that require a medium to travel through. The particles in a medium vibrate back and forth, transmitting the sound wave energy.

13. Sound cannot travel in space because there is no matter in space to transmit the sound waves. While planets and other celestial bodies may exist in space, they do not provide an adequate medium for sound waves to travel through. Additionally, the vast distances between celestial bodies means that any sound waves would quickly dissipate before reaching another object.
