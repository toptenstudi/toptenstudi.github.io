17. What are some of the linguistic considerations in age-related questions about SLA?​


Answer:

Second Language Acquisition (SLA) research on age-related questions has identified several linguistic considerations, including:

Age of onset: Research has shown that younger learners tend to have an advantage in SLA, as they are exposed to the second language at an earlier age and have more time to develop proficiency.

Critical period hypothesis: This hypothesis suggests that there is a critical period for language acquisition, and that after a certain age it becomes more difficult to acquire a second language.

Input and output: Age-related factors can influence the amount and type of input and output that learners are exposed to, which can impact their ability to acquire a second language.

Language aptitude: Some research suggests that older learners may have an advantage in certain aspects of language aptitude, such as metalinguistic awareness.

Motivation and attitude: Age-related factors can influence motivation and attitude towards learning a second language, which can impact the success of SLA.

Cognitive development: Age-related cognitive changes may influence the way learners process and acquire a second language.

Neural plasticity: Age-related factors may affect the brain's ability to adapt and change in response to new information, which can impact the success of SLA.

Interference: Age-related factors can also affect the extent of interference from the first language in second language acquisition.

It's important to remember that these are general tendencies and not applicable to every individual and these factors interact with each other in complex ways.

30. Why do researchers need to observe linguistic completeness and grammatical consistency in formulating questions?​


Grammatical structure is commonly assumed to exist in speakers' minds. However, grammatical structure is also directly involved in social interaction in language use, and language use is central to accounting for language acquisition, language variation and language change. In the more dynamic process of language acquisition and language change, functional factors have been argued to play a role. In both language acquisition and language change, it has been argued that competing motivations among functional principles play a major role.

The concept of competing motivations is that (functional) principles may come into conflict such that there is no grammatical system that satisfies all of the functional principles. As a result, change occurs over time in acquisition and in the history of a language, and the languages of the world exhibit structural diversity even though their speakers' linguistic behavior conforms to the same functional principles. (Competing motivation models do not presuppose that the competing principles are functional, and in fact competing motivation models are now used by many formalists; see below).
