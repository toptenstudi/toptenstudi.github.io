Answer:

1.Empirical research is research that is based on observation and measurement of phenomena, as directly experienced by the researcher. The data thus gathered may be compared against a theory or hypothesis, but the results are still based on real life experience.

2.Generally, research is the organised and systematic method of finding answers to questions. It is systematic because it is a process broken up into clear steps that lead to conclusions. Research is organised because there is a planned structure or method used to reach the conclusion.

3.Research, controlled: A study that compared results from a treated group and a control group. The control group may receive no treatment, a placebo, or a different treatment. See also blinded study, control group, double-blinded study.

4.The research hypothesis is central to all research endeavors, whether qualitative or quantitative, exploratory or explanatory. At its most basic, the research hypothesis states what the researcher expects to find – it is the tentative answer to the research question that guides the entire study.

5.Analytical research is a specific type of research that involves critical thinking skills and the evaluation of facts and information relative to the research being conducted. A variety of people including students, doctors and psychologists use analytical research during studies to find the most relevant information.

17. SUBJECT: *PRACTICAL RESEARCH*Can action research employ both quantitative and qualitative methods or approaches? Explain why or why not.​


Make me the brainliest :>

Answer:

Yes, action research can employ both quantitative and qualitative methods or approaches. In fact, this is one of the strengths of action research, as it allows researchers to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the problem or question being studied.

Quantitative methods involve collecting and analyzing numerical data, such as surveys, test scores, and statistical analyses. These methods are useful for obtaining objective data and identifying patterns or trends in the data. Quantitative data can also be used to measure the effectiveness of interventions and to compare different groups or conditions.

Qualitative methods, on the other hand, involve collecting and analyzing non-numerical data, such as interviews, observations, and open-ended survey questions. These methods are useful for obtaining subjective data and exploring the experiences, perspectives, and attitudes of participants. Qualitative data can also provide rich descriptions of the context and help researchers to understand the social and cultural dynamics of the problem being studied.

By employing both quantitative and qualitative methods, action research can provide a more nuanced and comprehensive understanding of the problem or question being studied. For example, a researcher studying student engagement in the classroom might use quantitative methods to collect data on attendance and test scores, as well as qualitative methods to conduct interviews with students and observe classroom interactions. By combining these data sources, the researcher can gain a deeper understanding of the factors that contribute to student engagement and identify effective strategies for improving it.
