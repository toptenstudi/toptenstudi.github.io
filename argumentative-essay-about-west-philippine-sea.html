3. Disagreeing the "No homework policy" argumentative essay for debate.Introduction:Argument1:Argument2:Argument3:Conclusion:​


Answer:

Introduction:

As students, we are accustomed to receiving homework as part of our academic life. However, the debate over the effectiveness of homework has been ongoing for years, with some advocating for the abolishment of homework altogether. While the idea of no homework policy may sound attractive to students, it is not a feasible solution to improve the educational system. In fact, homework is an essential component of learning, and it helps students develop crucial skills that they will need in the future.

Argument 1: Homework Helps Students Learn and Retain Information

One of the primary reasons why homework is essential is that it reinforces what students have learned in the classroom. By completing homework assignments, students are more likely to remember the information they have learned and retain it for a more extended period. Moreover, homework provides an opportunity for students to apply what they have learned in the classroom to real-life situations. This helps them develop a better understanding of the subject matter, and it enables them to see the practical applications of the knowledge they have gained.

Argument 2: Homework Develops Key Skills

Homework also helps students develop key skills that are essential for success in both their academic and personal lives. By completing homework assignments, students learn how to manage their time effectively, prioritize tasks, and meet deadlines. These are skills that will serve them well in college and beyond, as well as in their careers. Additionally, homework encourages students to think critically and independently, as they must solve problems on their own without the help of a teacher.

Argument 3: Homework Prepares Students for the Future

Homework is not just about learning subject matter; it is also about preparing students for the future. In college and the workforce, students will be required to complete assignments independently and manage their time effectively. Homework is an excellent way to develop these skills, and it provides students with a taste of what they can expect in the future. Additionally, homework teaches students responsibility and accountability, as they are expected to complete assignments on time and to the best of their abilities.

Conclusion:

While it may be tempting to do away with homework altogether, this is not a practical or effective solution. Homework is a critical component of learning, and it provides numerous benefits to students. It reinforces classroom learning, helps students develop key skills, and prepares them for the future. Rather than abolishing homework, we should focus on improving the quality of homework assignments and finding ways to make them more engaging and relevant to students' lives. Homework should be seen as an opportunity for students to continue their learning outside of the classroom, and as a way to develop essential skills that will serve them well in the future

6. 10 facts about West Philippine sea​


Answer:

The Philippine Sea is a marginal sea of the Western Pacific Ocean east of the Philippine archipelago (hence the name), occupying an estimated surface area of 5 million square kilometers (2×106 sq mi). The Philippine Sea Plate forms the floor of the sea. Its western border is the first island chain to the west, comprising the Ryukyu Islands in the northwest and Taiwan in the west. Its southwestern border comprises the Philippine islands of Luzon, Catanduanes, Samar, Leyte and Mindanao. Its northern border comprises the Japanese islands of Honshu, Shikoku and Kyūshū. Its eastern border is the second island chain to the west, comprising the Bonin Islands and Iwo Jima in the northeast, the Mariana Islands (including Guam, Saipan and Tinian) in the due east, and Halmahera, Palau, Yap and Ulithi (of the Caroline Islands) in the southeast. Its southern border is Indonesia's Morotai Island.

Answer:

The West Philippine Sea dispute is a contentious issue that involves several countries, including the Philippines, China, Taiwan, Vietnam, Malaysia, and Brunei. At the heart of the dispute is the overlapping territorial claims in the South China Sea, which is rich in natural resources and is strategically important for trade and security.

The Philippines claims the area within its exclusive economic zone (EEZ) based on the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS), which provides for a 200-nautical-mile EEZ from a country's coastline. China, on the other hand, claims nearly the entire South China Sea, including the West Philippine Sea, based on its historical nine-dash line claim, which is not recognized by UNCLOS.

China's aggressive actions in the West Philippine Sea, such as building artificial islands and military bases, conducting military drills, and harassing Filipino fishermen, have escalated tensions in the region. The Philippines has filed a case against China before an international tribunal, which ruled in 2016 that China's nine-dash line claim has no legal basis. However, China has rejected the ruling and continues to assert its claims in the area.

The West Philippine Sea dispute is a matter of national sovereignty and territorial integrity for the Philippines. It is essential for the Philippines to protect its rights and interests in the area and pursue peaceful and diplomatic means to resolve the dispute. Philippine Sea, section of the western North Pacific Ocean, lying east and north of the Philippines. The floor of this portion of the ocean is formed into a structural basin by a series of geologic folds and faults that protrude above the surface in the form of bordering island arcs. The Philippine islands of Luzon, Samar, and Mindanao are on the southwest; Palau, Yap, and Ulithi (of the Carolines) on the southeast; the Marianas, including Guam, Saipan, and Tinian, on the east; the Bonin and Volcano islands (Iwo Jima) on the northeast; the Japanese islands of Honshu, Shikoku, and Kyushu on the north; the Ryukyu Islands (Okinawa) on the northwest; and Taiwan (Formosa) in the extreme west. They surround an area measuring 1,800 miles (2,900 km) north-south by 1,500 miles east-west and occupying a total surface area of 40,000 square miles (1,000,000 square km), about 3 percent of the entire Pacific region. The basin, with a general depth of 19,700 feet (6,000 m), plunges to its greatest depths in trenches to the east of the island arcs. The deepest is the Philippine Trench at 34,578 feet (10,539 m). Numerous seamounts rise from the basin floor, some of which are volcanic; their peaks, often flat (called tablemounts, or guyots), are capped with coral. The warm Pacific North Equatorial Current flows westward across the southern part of the sea. On meeting the Philippines, the current divides; part swings north near Luzon to form the Kuroshio (q.v.; Japan Current), of which some will return to the sea as the Kuroshio Countercurrent, and part swings south as the Pacific Equatorial Countercurrent. These currents, together with areas near reefs, ridges, and seamounts, are the sites of fishing grounds. Typhoons, which become particularly strong in September, originate in the sea.

The Philippines has been ranked as the fifth most mineralised country in the world, with an estimated US$1 trillion in untapped reserves of copper, gold, nickel, zinc and silver. Despite the foregoing, statistics from the Philippine Mines and Geosciences Bureau (MGB) indicate that, as of 2017, there are only 50 operating mines. Target minerals

What are the target minerals?

The Philippines’ top mineral exports are copper, gold and nickel. Other target minerals include quartz, mica, iron, gypsum, feldspar, chromite, calcite and sulphur. Some target non-metallic minerals are sand and gravel, limestone, marble, clay and other quarry materials.

Cobalt is the main factor for the increased interest in minerals used in battery technology in the Philippines. The Philippines has the fourth-largest cobalt reserves worldwide, at an estimated 280,000 tonnes.
