3. In light of Aristotle’s discussion about the highest purpose and the ultimate good, explain why happiness or eudaimonia is not simply having wealth, power, and pleasurable feelings.​


Aristotle believed that the highest purpose of a human life is eudaimonia, which can be translated as happiness or fulfillment. However, he argued that happiness is not simply having wealth, power, or pleasurable feelings. Instead, it is a state of well-being that arises from living a virtuous life, which involves fulfilling one's natural potential and living in accordance with reason.

Aristotle believed that the human being is a rational animal, meaning that their primary characteristic is the ability to reason and act rationally. He argued that living a virtuous life means developing one's abilities and fulfilling one's potential as a rational being, which involves cultivating moral virtues such as courage, temperance, generosity, and wisdom. These virtues are essential for living a happy and fulfilling life because they enable one to make the right decisions and live in harmony with others.

Furthermore, Aristotle rejected the idea that happiness is simply having material wealth or power, as he believed that these things can lead to corruption and misery. Instead, he argued that happiness is achieved through the pursuit of wisdom and virtue, which involve living in harmony with oneself and others, pursuing intellectual and moral excellence, and finding meaning and purpose in life.

In conclusion, while wealth, power, and pleasurable feelings may contribute to well-being and happiness, they are not the ultimate good or purpose of human life according to Aristotle. Instead, living a virtuous and fulfilling life, which involves developing moral virtues and fulfilling one's natural potential as a rational being, is the highest purpose and ultimate good.
