19. why does the amount of heat received by places for from the equator become less?​


Answer:

The amount of heat received by places farther from the equator becomes less due to the angle at which the sun's rays strike the Earth's surface.

At the equator, the sun's rays are nearly perpendicular to the surface of the Earth, resulting in a more direct and intense heat. This is because the equator is closer to the sun and the angle of incidence of the sun's rays is smaller, leading to a larger concentration of energy per unit area.

However, as you move away from the equator towards the poles, the angle of incidence of the sun's rays becomes more oblique. This means that the same amount of energy is spread over a larger area, resulting in less concentrated heat. This is why places closer to the poles experience cooler temperatures.

Additionally, the Earth's curvature also plays a role in the amount of heat received. The curvature of the Earth causes the sun's rays to spread out over a larger area as they travel from the equator towards the poles. This results in a reduction of heat per unit area.

These factors together cause the amount of heat received by places to decrease as they move farther away from the equator

30. why does the amount of heat receive by places far from the equatoe become less?​


Answer:

The amount of heat received by places far from the equator becomes less because of the way the Earth is tilted on its axis. The Earth's axis is tilted at an angle of approximately 23.5 degrees with respect to the plane of its orbit around the sun. This tilt causes the angle at which the sun's rays hit the Earth's surface to vary throughout the year.

When the Earth is tilted towards the sun, as it is during the summer in the Northern Hemisphere, the sun's rays are more concentrated and direct, which results in more heat being received. Conversely, when the Earth is tilted away from the sun, as it is during the winter in the Northern Hemisphere, the sun's rays are more spread out and less concentrated, which results in less heat being received.

In addition to this seasonal variation, the amount of heat received by places far from the equator is also affected by the angle at which the sun's rays strike the Earth's surface. Places closer to the equator receive more direct sunlight throughout the year, while places farther away from the equator receive sunlight at an oblique angle, which results in less heat being received.
