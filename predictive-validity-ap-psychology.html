7. 10. What is the type of validity when an instrument produces results similar to those ofanother instrument that will be employed in the future?A. Predictive ValidityC. Content ValidityB. Criterion ValidityD. Face Validity​


A. Predictive Validity

THE PREDICTIVE VALIDITY

The degree to which test scores accurately predict scores on a criterion measure is its predictive validity. The extent to which college admissions test scores predict college grade point average is a striking example (GPA).

The predictive validity of an assessment is established by measuring the correlation coefficient between the assessment findings and the subsequent behavior of interest. The stronger the correlation between the assessment data and the target behavior, the higher the degree of predictive validity the assessment possesses.

In the context of pre-employment testing, predictive validity relates to how likely it is for test scores to predict the future performance of the company. Predictive validity is one type of validity evidence, which is a method for confirming a test's correlation with concrete outcomes.

Predictive validity in psychometrics is the degree to which a score on a scale or test predicts scores on a criterion measure. The validity of a cognitive test for job performance, for instance, is determined by the correlation between test scores and, for instance, supervisor performance ratings.

Predictive validity is illustrated when a test can anticipate a future outcome. To establish this type of validity, the test must correlate with a variable that can only be evaluated in the future, for example, after administration.

13. Operationalize the five ways of establishing validity in measurement by giving each two (2) exampleWays of Establishing Validity 1. Face Validity 2. Content Validity3. Predictive Validity4. Construct Validity5. Convergent Validityexample1.2.3.4.5.​


Validity tells you how accurately a method measures something. The types of validity mentioned are construct, content, face, criterion, convergent, and prediction validity that will be elaborated below.

Construct validity

Example:

We cannot directly measure "depression" as an objective, observable thing. However, based on current psychology theory and research, we are able to quantify depression using a range of signs and symptoms, such as low energy and self-confidence.

Content validity

Example:

An end-of-semester algebra test is created by a math instructor for her class. Every type of algebra that was covered in class should be covered on the test. If particular forms of algebra are omitted, the findings could not be a reliable reflection of how well pupils comprehend the material. Likewise, if she adds questions that have nothing to do with algebra, the findings are no longer a reliable indicator of algebra proficiency.

Face validity

Example:

You design a poll to gauge how regularly people practice their eating habits. You look over the survey items, which include inquiries regarding each day's meals as well as any between-meal snacks. The survey appears to be a good depiction of what you want to test on the surface, therefore you value its high face validity.

Criterion validity

Example:

A university professor develops a brand-new exam to gauge candidates' proficiency in English writing. She locates an existing test that is thought to be a valid measurement of English writing ability and analyzes the results when the same set of students take both tests in order to determine how effectively the test actually measures students' writing skill. The new test has high criterion validity when the results are strikingly comparable.

Convergent validity

Example:

Use both participant observation and a survey as measurements. It is clear that they are measuring the same construct if the results of your two measurements are comparable (i.e., they converge).

Predictive validity

Example:

If people who score highly on an honesty test later demonstrate their honesty by their conduct, the exam has predictive validity.
