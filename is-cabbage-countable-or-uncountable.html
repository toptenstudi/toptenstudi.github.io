25. definition and example of countable and uncountable nouns for reporting po​


Answer:

Countable nouns are nouns that refer to individual objects or items that can be counted, for example: book, chair, dog, car, etc. Countable nouns have a singular and plural form and can be used with numbers and articles (a, an, the).

Uncountable nouns are nouns that refer to substances, concepts, or things that cannot be counted or divided into individual units, for example: water, air, music, love, etc. Uncountable nouns do not have a plural form and are typically used with singular verbs. They cannot be used with articles (a, an, the) and are usually referred to with the indefinite article “some” or the definite article “the”.

Examples:

Countable nouns:

I have three books.

She bought a new chair for her office.

Uncountable nouns:

She drank some water.

The music was soothing.

30. what do we call the practice of limiting the meaning of countable and uncountable nouns? a. limiting adjectives b. Determiners C. modifiers


Explanation:

The practice of limiting the meaning of countable and uncountable nouns is called "determiners". Determiners are a type of word that is used before a noun to indicate the reference of the noun in the context, and to limit the meaning of the noun. Examples of determiners include articles (a, an, the), demonstratives (this, that, these, those), possessives (my, your, his, her, its, our, their), and quantifiers (some, any, few, several, many, much, little, etc.).

Limiting adjectives and modifiers can also be used to provide additional information about a noun, but they do not have the specific function of limiting the reference of the noun in the way that determiners do.
