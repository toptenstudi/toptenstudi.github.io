What are exoplanets?

Answer:

Exoplanets are planets that orbit a star other than our sun. The prefix Exo comes from Greek and means outside; these worlds are far, far outside our own solar system. Astronomers have confirmed more than 4,000 exoplanets orbiting distant stars, with at least 1,000 more awaiting confirmation. They found the first two exoplanets orbiting a pulsar in 1992. They confirmed the first exoplanet orbiting a sunlike stain in 1995. Prior to those discoveries, the existence of planetary systems other than our own had been surmised for centuries and in the early 20th century a planet was heralded for Barnard’s star (it later turned out to be spurious). The true discoveries came with advancing technology, a relatively recent development.

Why didn’t we see them before?

It’s because exoplanets are so far away, several light-years away at their closest. And it’s because – unlike stars – exoplanets don’t shine with their own light. Like our own Earth, they shine only with light reflected from their local stars. In contrast to their stars, exoplanets are exceedingly dim; even the largest are drowned in the light of their vastly brighter stars. That’s why, even now, even the largest are too small to be glimpsed in optical telescopes, although a few have been directly imaged.

Before the first exoplanet discovery, most astronomers assumed exoplanets, if found, would resemble the planets in our solar system. The great shock has been that many exoplanets are very different, with their positions and orbits difficult to explain. If astronomers thought the solar system was in any way representative of other planetary systems out there in the galaxy, they’ve been disappointed. Our solar system may be the exception rather than the rule.

Why are they important?

Studying the diverse range of exoplanets and planetary systems that have been discovered to date – from the small to the large, from those that appear Earth-like to the profoundly bizarre – not only helps us learn about how these particular systems formed and evolved, but provides essential clues towards understanding whether and where life might exist elsewhere in the Universe.

23. What is the difference between reinforced concrete and prestressed concrete?​


Answer:

Reinforced concrete and prestressed concrete are both types of concrete that have reinforcing materials added to improve their strength and durability. However, there are some key differences between these two types of concrete.

Reinforced concrete is made by adding steel bars or mesh into the wet concrete mix before it sets. This reinforcing material helps to improve the tensile strength of the concrete, which is otherwise relatively weak. When the concrete sets and hardens, the steel reinforcing bars or mesh become embedded in the concrete, creating a composite material that has high compressive strength and good resistance to cracking and other types of damage.

Prestressed concrete, on the other hand, is made by adding high-strength steel wires or cables into the concrete that are already under tension before the concrete is poured. The concrete is then allowed to set and harden around the pre-tensioned wires or cables, which helps to increase its compressive strength and reduce its tendency to crack. The tension in the wires or cables is released after the concrete has set, causing them to pull the concrete inwards and creating a compressive force within the concrete that makes it even stronger.

In summary, the main difference between reinforced concrete and prestressed concrete is in how the reinforcing material is added to the concrete. Reinforced concrete has reinforcing bars or mesh added before the concrete is poured, while prestressed concrete has high-strength wires or cables added that are already under tension before the concrete is poured. This difference in construction methods gives each type of concrete unique properties and advantages for different applications.
