What are the five senses of the human body?

Sensory receptors take in environmental stimuli, transmit signals to various parts of the brain, and the brain processes and interprets those signals as sight, sound, touch, smell, or taste. Different organs and types of receptors are used by each sense.

1. Taste

The tongue has hundreds of taste buds that detect five flavors: sweet, salty, bitter, sour, and umami. The gustatory cortex processes the signals that are provided by the taste buds. Flavor is produced by combining taste and smell. Because of this, food "tastes" different when your nose is congested.

2. Smell

At the upper regions of the nose, there are a lot of receptors. Mucus-covered cilia, which resemble microscopic hairs and react to "smelly" chemicals, are used by these olfactory receptors to detect smell. These signals are received and processed by the olfactory cortex, which is located at the front of the brain.

3. Sight

On the retina at the retinal back of the eye are the sight receptors. Cones are used for seeing in color, while rods are used for seeing in black and white. The occipital cortex processes vision. Left visual field signals are processed by the right side of the visual cortex, and right visual field signals are processed by the left visual cortex.

4. Hearing

Hearing includes a long series of procedures, but it all culminates in the cochlea, a spiral-shaped canal in the inner ear, where the mechanical input is converted to an electrical signal and sent to the auditory cortex in the brain's cortex.

5. Touch

The body's largest organ is the skin. It has numerous millions of touch receptors that react to pressure, temperature, pain, and touch. This information is processed by the somatosensory cortex. Fingertips, lips, and eyelids contain more of these receptors.
