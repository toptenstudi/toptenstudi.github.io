1. . Explain the features and differences of bipartite and tripartite view of Philippine history


Bipartite and tripartite talks are frequently referred to as such in accordance with labor legislation in Philippine history. Both are aimed at resolving labor relations or industrial relations conflicts between businesses, employers, or employers and employees. Bipartite negotiations are talks between employees or trade unions and employers to settle industrial relations complaints, also known as the Law. Tripartite discussions, on the other hand, involve the mediation of labor conflicts by outside parties.

Bipartite negotiations are conducted in the first stage, which must be finished within thirty days of the beginning of discussions, and are carried out by both parties, employers and employees, or workers or labor unions and employees. Tripartite talks, or discussions involving the assistance of other parties to address the issue, are the second stage or the necessary action to be performed after the bipartite negotiations are deemed unsuccessful.

27. Tawag sa alyansa ng tripartite pact sa pagitan ng Japan, Germany,Italy at ng dalawa pang militaristikong bansa. ​.


Answer:

The Tripartite Pact was, together with the Anti-Comintern Pact and the Pact of Steel, one of several agreements between Germany, Japan, Italy, and other countries of the Axis Powers governing their relationship.

The Tripartite Pact, also known as the Berlin Pact, was an agreement between Germany, Italy, and Japan signed in Berlin on 27 September 1940 by, respectively, Joachim von Ribbentrop, Galeazzo Ciano, and Saburō Kurusu. It was a defensive military alliance that was eventually joined by Hungary (20 November 1940), Romania (23 November 1940), Bulgaria (1 March 1941), and Yugoslavia (25 March 1941) as well as by the German client state of Slovakia (24 November 1940). Yugoslavia's accession provoked a coup d'état in Belgrade two days later. Germany, Italy, and Hungary responded by invading Yugoslavia. The resulting Italo-German client state, known as the Independent State of Croatia, joined the pact on 15 June 1941.
