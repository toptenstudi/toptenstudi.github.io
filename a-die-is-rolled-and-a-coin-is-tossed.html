4. write the sample of space Tossing of two coins and rolling a die​


Answer:

For example, tossing a coin has 2 items in its sample space. Rolling a die has 6. Thus, the sample space of the experiment from simultaneously flipping a coin and rolling a die consisted of: 2 × 6 = 12 possible outcomes.

Step-by-step explanation:

A sample space is the set of all possible outcomes of a random experiment. When you toss a coin, there are only two possible outcomes-heads (h) or tails (t) so the sample space for the coin toss experiment is {h,t} .

Rolling two six-sided dice: Each die has 6 equally likely outcomes, so the sample space is 6 • 6 or 36 equally likely outcomes. Flipping three coins: Each coin has 2 equally likely outcomes, so the sample space is 2 • 2 • 2 or 8 equally likely outcomes.

15. Joey rolls a six-sided die, and then tosses a coin. What is the probability of rolling a prime number and tossing a head?


Step-by-step explanation:

There are 3 prime numbers (2, 3, and 5) on a six-sided die, and there are 2 possible outcomes when tossing a coin (heads or tails).

The probability of rolling a prime number is 3/6 or 1/2, since there are 3 prime numbers out of 6 possible outcomes.

The probability of tossing a head is 1/2, since there are 2 possible outcomes, and only 1 of them results in a head.

To find the probability of both events happening, we multiply the probabilities together:

P(rolling a prime number and tossing a head) = P(rolling a prime number) x P(tossing a head)

P(rolling a prime number and tossing a head) = (1/2) x (1/2)

P(rolling a prime number and tossing a head) = 1/4

Therefore, the probability of rolling a prime number and tossing a head is 1/4 or 0.25.

25. a coin is tossed and die is rolled simultinose for 6 time s​


Answer:

On the assumptions that (a) the die is an unbiased six-sided die (not weighted so that it favors one side more than another, (b) the coin is an unbiased two-sided coin (so that H and T are equally likely to occur), and (c) the coin flip and die roll are probabilistically independent of one another (so that the outcome of one does not affect the likelihood of the outcome of the other), the probability is 1/6 * 1/2, or 1/12.

If either the die or the coin (or both) is biased but the independence assumption still holds, the methodology is the same, but the resulting probability is different. E.g., suppose the die is weighted in favor of 6 so that a 6 comes up 1/4th of the time rather than 1/6th, and the coin is biased 60/40 in favor of heads, the probability you’re looking for is 1/4*(0.6), or 0.15.

If either the die or the coin (or both) is biased, but you don’t know the magnitude of the bias, or if the independence assumption doesn’t hold, then you’re SOL in terms of calculating the probability. You’d need more data.

30. Rolling a die and tossing a coin simultaneously


Answer:

Rolling a die and tossing a coin simultaneously would result in a sample space of 12 possible outcomes, assuming that the die has six faces numbered 1 to 6, and the coin has two possible outcomes (heads or tails).

The possible outcomes can be represented by pairs of numbers, where the first number represents the result of rolling the die, and the second number represents the result of tossing the coin. The 12 possible outcomes are:

(1, H), (1, T), (2, H), (2, T), (3, H), (3, T), (4, H), (4, T), (5, H), (5, T), (6, H), (6, T)

Each of these outcomes is equally likely to occur, assuming that the die and the coin are fair and unbiased.
